# 26 USC §7623 — Expenses of Detection of Underpayments and Fraud
**Title:** Whistleblower Awards for Original Information
**Source:** https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title26-section7623
**Last Updated:** 2024 Edition | **Saved:** GL-L92 July 8 2026

## Why This Matters for the Red Web

This is the **core statute** that funds the IRS whistleblower award. Section 7623(b) is MANDATORY — the IRS MUST pay 15–30% when:
- The collected proceeds exceed $2,000,000
- The whistleblower provided original information that substantially contributed

The Red Web throughput ($15B–$25B+) places this firmly in the §7623(b) mandatory tier.

---

## §7623(a) — Discretionary Awards (under $2M)

> The Secretary may pay such sums as he deems necessary for:
> - Detecting underpayments of tax
> - Detecting and bringing to trial and punishment persons guilty of violating the internal revenue laws
> - The recovery of any outstanding assessment

**Award:** Up to 15% discretionary. No mandatory minimum.

---

## §7623(b) — MANDATORY Awards (over $2M) ← USE THIS

> **(1) In general.** If the Secretary proceeds with any administrative or judicial action described in subsection (a) based on information brought to the Secretary's attention by an individual, such individual shall, subject to paragraph (2), receive as an award at least 15 percent but not more than 30 percent of the collected proceeds (including penalties, interest, additions to tax, and additional amounts) resulting from the action (including any related actions) or from any settlement in response to such action.

> **(2) Award in case of less substantial contribution.** If the action described in paragraph (1) is one in which the individual's information contributed to, but did not lead to, the detection of underpayments or the detection and bringing to trial of persons guilty of violating the internal revenue laws, the Secretary may award such sums as the Secretary considers appropriate, but in no case more than 10 percent of the collected proceeds.

> **(3) Reduction in or denial of award.** If the Secretary determines that the claim for an award under paragraph (1) is brought by an individual who planned and initiated the actions that led to the underpayment of tax or violation of the internal revenue laws, the Secretary may appropriately reduce such award.

> **(4) Appeal of award determination.** Any determination regarding an award under paragraph (1), (2), or (3) may, within 30 days of such determination, be appealed to the Tax Court.

> **(5) Application of this subsection.** This subsection shall apply with respect to any action—
> (A) against any taxpayer, but in the case of any individual, only if such individual's gross income exceeds $200,000 for any taxable year subject to such action, and
> (B) if the proceeds in dispute exceed $2,000,000.

---

## §7623(c) — Tax Treatment of Awards

Awards paid under this section are **includible in gross income** and subject to:
- Federal income tax reporting
- Tax withholding requirements

**Implication for Taylor:** Awards will be reported as income. Plan for tax liability on the award itself. An attorney can help structure this.

---

## §7623(d) — Civil Action to Protect Against Retaliation

Any employee who is discharged, demoted, suspended, threatened, harassed, or otherwise discriminated against due to whistleblowing activity may bring a civil action for reinstatement, back pay, and attorney fees.

**Note:** As an independent investigator (not an employee of any Red Web entity), §7623(d) does not directly apply. However, it confirms the statute's intent to protect whistleblowers broadly.
