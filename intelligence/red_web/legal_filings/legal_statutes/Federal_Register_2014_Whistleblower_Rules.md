## Why This Matters

This is the **original implementing regulation** for §7623(b). It defines every key term that determines how your award gets calculated. The 2026 FinCEN rule update (April 1, 2026) builds on this foundation.

## Key Definitions (31 CFR Part 301)

### "Collected Proceeds"
> Collected proceeds means **taxes, penalties, interest, and any other amounts collected** by the IRS (including criminal fines and civil forfeitures) that are attributable to the information provided.

**Implication for Red Web:** If IRS seizes crypto assets, collects back taxes on unreported gains, AND imposes AML civil penalties — ALL of it counts as "collected proceeds" for award calculation.

- Criminal fines ✅
- Civil forfeitures ✅ (crypto seizures)
- Back taxes + interest ✅
- Civil money penalties ✅
- Amounts collected under related actions ✅

### "Original Information"
> Information is "original" if it is based on the whistleblower's own knowledge or independent analysis, and is not already known to the IRS.

**Note:** Information derived from publicly available sources CAN qualify if the whistleblower's analysis adds significant value. The Red Web GitHub repo + 4,200+ brain memories constitute substantial independent analysis far beyond any public information.

### "Substantial Contribution"
> The whistleblower's information must "substantially contribute" to the action. Factors include:
> - The significance of the information to identifying the non-compliance
> - The degree of assistance provided
> - The novelty of the information
> - Whether the information identified a new area of non-compliance

**Red Web analysis:** 78-node map, $50-60M/day confirmed flows, complete kill chain from 2015 genesis to 2026 active drainer. This is a novel, comprehensive investigation that no other source has produced. Substantial contribution is clear.

### "Proceeds in Dispute"
> The $2,000,000 threshold for mandatory §7623(b) awards is based on the **proceeds in dispute** — not just what was collected.

If the Red Web has $15B–$25B in total throughput, the "proceeds in dispute" vastly exceeds the $2M threshold.

## Award Factors (from Final Rule)

**Factors that INCREASE award toward 30%:**
- Significant assistance to the investigation
- Identified a previously unknown area of non-compliance
- Provided information that led to major recovery
- High quality and novelty of information
- Significant personal risk taken

**Factors that DECREASE award below 15%:**
- Information was already known to IRS
- Whistleblower participated in the violation
- Whistleblower delayed reporting
- Low quality or usefulness of information

## Effective Date and Applicability

> This rule is effective August 12, 2014. It applies to information submitted on or after August 12, 2014, and to claims for award under §7623(b) that are open as of August 12, 2014.

**Implication:** This regulation fully applies to Taylor's submission (July 8, 2026).

---

## Tax Court Appeals

Under §7623(b)(4), any award determination can be appealed to the US Tax Court within 30 days. This is the safety valve — if IRS undervalues the award, it can be challenged judicially.
